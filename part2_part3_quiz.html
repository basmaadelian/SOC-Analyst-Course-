PART 2 — Security Controls  (Q01 – Q13)
Q01MFA on email only — attacker logs into cloud storage with stolen credentials.
✓ Correct Answer: A — Defense in depth violated
Defense in depth requires layering controls across all access points, not just the most obvious one. MFA on email is good, but an attacker who steals credentials will simply try every other system where MFA is absent. Every privileged pathway — VPN, cloud storage, backup console, admin portals — needs the same preventive controls. Attackers specifically look for the unprotected path. This is why real-world deployments must audit all access vectors, not just the primary login portal.
Q02SIEM rule fires: login from two countries within 30 minutes.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Detective control correlating multiple events
The SIEM rule is a detective control — it does not block the login, it identifies that something suspicious has occurred. Its value is correlation: a single login from a foreign IP might be noise, but two logins from different countries within 30 minutes is an impossible travel pattern that strongly suggests credential compromise. This is the engineer's insight: individual events are often ambiguous; it is the combination of events that reveals malicious intent. The SIEM's job is to connect those dots faster than a human reviewer could manually.
Q03Ransomware reinfects systems two days after restoration from backup.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Systems restored before patching the original vulnerability
This is one of the most common and costly mistakes in incident response. Restoring from backup returns the system to a working state — but if the vulnerability that allowed the initial attack is still present, the attacker can simply exploit it again within hours. The correct sequence is: contain and isolate first, then eradicate the threat and patch the vulnerability, then restore from clean backups, then verify the restored environment is clean before reconnecting. Skipping the patch step guarantees reinfection.
Q04"CCTV in operation" sign causes attacker to target a less-protected competitor.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Deterrent control; ineffective against determined attackers
The sign worked in this scenario — it deterred an opportunistic attacker who chose an easier target. That is exactly what deterrent controls are designed to do: raise the perceived cost or risk of an attack. The limitation is equally important: nation-state actors, organized criminal groups, and targeted attackers have already accepted the legal and operational risks involved. A sign will not stop someone who has decided the target is worth the risk. Deterrent controls are most effective against low-sophistication, opportunistic threats.
Q05Legacy medical device on Windows XP — cannot be patched or replaced for 18 months.
✓ Correct Answer: B — VLAN isolation, restricted connections, enhanced monitoring
This is the classic compensating controls scenario. The ideal control — patching — is unavailable. The engineer's job is not to declare the situation unacceptable and walk away; it is to reduce risk to an acceptable level through alternative measures. VLAN isolation limits lateral movement if the device is compromised. Restricting inbound connections to only authorized devices reduces the attack surface. Enhanced monitoring on that segment ensures any anomalous activity is detected quickly. Password rotation alone (Option D) does nothing about the unpatched OS vulnerability.
Q06SIEM stops receiving firewall logs for 3 weeks — attacker goes undetected.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Blind spot in log coverage
A SIEM that is not receiving logs is not detecting anything — but it may appear to be working normally from the dashboard. This is one of the most dangerous failure modes because it creates a false sense of security. The organization believed its detective control was functioning when it had a critical blind spot. Log source health monitoring — verifying that every source is actively sending data and that the data volume is within expected ranges — is part of an analyst's ongoing operational responsibility. A rule that references a dead log source is not a detection; it is an open gap.
Q07Annual security training, AUP signing, and background checks.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Administrative controls
Administrative controls (also called managerial controls) govern how people behave and how processes are executed. They do not involve hardware, software, or physical barriers — they involve policy, procedure, and human governance. Training changes what employees know and do. The AUP defines expectations and provides a legal basis for enforcement. Background checks reduce insider threat risk before a person enters the environment. All three operate through organizational process and human behavior, which is the defining characteristic of the administrative category.
Q08Attacker tailgates through door, plugs rogue device into conference room network port.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Physical controls failure
Once an attacker has physical presence inside the building, they bypass every network-layer control ever configured. The firewall cannot stop a device that is already connected on the inside of the network. The IDS may eventually detect suspicious traffic, but the initial access was physical. Mantraps, tailgating detection, visitor sign-in procedures, security guard presence, and port security (disabling unused network ports) are all physical and administrative controls that would have prevented or detected this attack. Students who only think in technical controls will configure excellent firewalls and watch attackers walk right past them.
Q09Why is security awareness training a cost-effective preventive control?
✓ Correct Answer: B — Attacks the root cause of 95% of breaches: human error
IBM Security research consistently finds that human error is involved in approximately 95% of breaches — phishing clicks, weak passwords, mishandled data, shadow IT decisions. If humans are the root cause of most incidents, then changing human behavior through training is a direct attack on the root cause. Training is cost-effective because the investment is relatively low (compared to technical controls) and the coverage is broad — every trained employee is a potential line of defense against phishing, social engineering, and credential misuse. It does not replace technical controls; it complements them.
Q10Why does network segmentation make incident response containment faster?
✓ Correct Answer: B — Segmented networks allow isolation by configuration change
In a segmented network, each segment is logically isolated by design. Quarantining a compromised segment means adjusting firewall rules or VLAN configuration — a fast, targeted action. In a flat network where every device can reach every other device, containment requires tracing every potential lateral connection the attacker could use, blocking them individually, and hoping nothing is missed. This is slow, error-prone under pressure, and often incomplete. This is the direct connection between what students learned in Module 1 (network design) and what they will practice in Module 6 (incident response) — good architecture makes security operations easier.
Q11Identify the category of: policy, AES-256/TLS, badge reader.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Administrative / Technical / Physical
The policy is an administrative control — it governs expected behavior through an organizational rule. AES-256 and TLS are technical controls — they are implemented in hardware and software and enforce the policy automatically. The badge reader is a physical control — it restricts access through a tangible barrier in the real world. This question illustrates how all three categories can be in place protecting the same asset simultaneously, each addressing a different attack surface. A mature security program integrates all three.
Q12IDS generates 50,000 unreviewed alerts per day — attacker undetected for 4 months.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Unreviewed alerts are noise, not detection
A detective control has two components: the tool that generates the signal and the human process that acts on it. An IDS that fires 50,000 alerts per day with no analyst capacity to review them provides zero real detection capability — it just creates the appearance of security. This is called alert fatigue, and it is one of the most common failure modes in security programs. The solution is not simply more tools — it is tuning detection rules to reduce noise, prioritizing high-fidelity alerts, and ensuring analyst capacity matches alert volume. Detection is a system, not just software.
Q13Most accurate statement about compensating controls from an engineering perspective.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Untested compensating controls are documented assumptions, not real controls
This is the engineering discipline that separates competent professionals from those who just check boxes. A compensating control that looks good on paper but has never been verified under realistic conditions provides only the illusion of security. If the VLAN isolation has a misconfigured ACL that allows traffic you assumed was blocked, the compensating control is not working — and you will only discover this when an attacker exploits the gap. Regular testing — penetration testing, firewall rule audits, simulated lateral movement — is what transforms documented compensating controls into verified security posture.
PART 3 — Risk Management & Governance  (Q14 – Q25)
Q14SQL injection vulnerability on public app storing credit card data — prioritization.
✓ Correct Answer: C — High risk; all three components are elevated
Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Impact. Threat: SQL injection is actively and widely exploited — threat actors have automated tools for it. Vulnerability: confirmed, unpatched, and publicly accessible. Impact: credit card data exposure triggers PCI DSS penalties, breach notification costs, fraud liability, and reputational damage. All three components are at maximum. "It hasn't been exploited yet" is not a risk assessment — it is hope. The absence of a known incident does not reduce the risk of a future one. This combination demands immediate remediation.
Q15AV = $200,000 · EF = 80% · ARO = 0.5 → ALE = ?
✓ Correct Answer: A — $80,000
The calculation: SLE = AV × EF = $200,000 × 0.80 = $160,000. ALE = SLE × ARO = $160,000 × 0.5 = $80,000. The ARO of 0.5 means the event is expected once every two years — so the annual expected loss is half of the single-event loss. This $80,000 ALE is now the rational ceiling for annual spending on controls to prevent this incident. Spending $120,000 per year to prevent an $80,000 annual loss is not financially rational. This is how quantitative risk assessment drives proportionate security investment decisions.
Q16Same vulnerability on isolated dev server vs. payment server — how to prioritize.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Prioritize the payment server
The same CVE on two different systems does not represent equal risk. Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Impact. Both servers have the same vulnerability. But the payment server has a much higher threat exposure (internet-accessible, actively targeted by attackers) and much higher impact (payment card data, regulatory penalties). The dev server is isolated with no sensitive data — low threat exposure, low impact. This is asset-driven prioritization: knowing which assets matter most is what allows you to invest remediation effort where it reduces the most risk. Treating all vulnerabilities equally is how teams get overwhelmed and miss what matters.
Q17Stop storing card data entirely — use tokenization service instead.
✓ Correct Answer: D — Risk avoidance
Risk avoidance means eliminating the activity that creates the risk. If the company no longer stores payment card data — because the data flows directly to a tokenization provider and never touches company systems — the risk of a card data breach is avoided entirely. This is more complete than mitigation (which would mean keeping the data but adding controls around it). It is different from transfer (which would mean keeping the risk but shifting the financial cost to an insurer). Avoidance is appropriate when the residual risk after mitigation is still unacceptably high, and when the activity is not essential to the core business mission.
Q18CISO formally documents acceptance of patch risk after evaluation.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Deliberate, documented, accountable decision = governance
The difference between negligence and governance is process and accountability. Negligence is not patching because no one got around to it — no evaluation, no documentation, no accountability. Governance is evaluating the risk, comparing it against the cost and disruption of mitigation, making a deliberate decision, documenting the rationale, and having an accountable leader sign off. The CISO's signature creates a formal record that the risk was consciously accepted rather than overlooked. For analysts, this distinction matters: a vulnerability scanner finding against a system with a documented risk acceptance is a known accepted risk — escalating it generates noise. One without documentation requires immediate escalation.
Q19Cyber insurance pays after breach, but same vulnerability causes second breach.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Transfer shifts cost, not likelihood or impact
Risk transfer is a financial mechanism, not a security mechanism. Cyber insurance pays the bill after an incident occurs — it does not make the incident less likely to occur, and it does not reduce the damage to systems, data, or customers. The underlying vulnerability remained after the first breach because no one patched it. Buying insurance and doing nothing else is one of the most dangerous postures an organization can adopt, because it creates financial comfort while leaving the actual risk completely intact. Transfer works best as a complement to mitigation — covering residual risk after controls have reduced likelihood and impact as much as practical.
Q20SOC analyst investigates SIEM alert about lateral movement — which NIST functions?
✓ Correct Answer: C — Detect and Respond
The Detect function covers capabilities to identify when an incident occurs — SIEM alerts are the primary output of this function. The Respond function covers taking action once a detection fires — investigation, analysis, containment, and eradication. Both apply simultaneously during active incident investigation. Identify covers asset inventory and risk assessment (done before incidents). Protect covers preventive safeguards. Recover covers restoration after the incident is resolved. Mapping daily analyst work to NIST functions is a useful mental model for understanding where each activity fits in the overall security lifecycle.
Q21European enterprise wants formal, third-party certified ISMS assurance.
✓ Correct Answer: C — ISO 27001
ISO 27001 is the only option listed that requires a formal two-stage audit by an accredited third party leading to certification. NIST CSF is a flexible risk framework with no certification process. CIS Controls are a prioritized control baseline with no certification. PCI DSS is a compliance standard specific to payment card data, not a general ISMS certification. ISO 27001 provides internationally recognized, third-party verified evidence of a mature information security management system — which is specifically what European customers and partners often require in vendor contracts and procurement processes.
Q22Target and Equifax: compliant but breached. What does this conclude?
✓ Correct Answer: B — Compliance is a minimum baseline, not a security guarantee
The pattern across Target, Equifax, Capital One, and SolarWinds is consistent: compliance defines the minimum floor, not the security ceiling. PCI DSS covers payment card processing systems — it said nothing about the HVAC vendor network (Target) or the unpatched Apache Struts server on a non-payment system (Equifax). Standards are written to establish baselines, not to guarantee security. A mature security program meets every compliance obligation as a starting point, then adds continuous monitoring, threat intelligence, red team exercises, and controls proportional to actual risk — not just to auditor checklists. Compliance is a legal and regulatory requirement. Security is what actually protects the organization.
Q23Where does "TLS 1.2 or higher" belong in the policy hierarchy?
✓ Correct Answer: B — In the Standard
Standards translate policy intent into specific, mandatory technical requirements. The policy says "protect data in transit" — that is the what and the why. The standard says "use TLS 1.2 or higher" — that is the specific, mandatory how. Putting "TLS 1.2" directly in the policy would be a mistake because when TLS 1.2 is eventually deprecated (as TLS 1.0 and 1.1 already have been), the entire policy document would need to be rewritten and re-approved by leadership. Keeping it in the standard means a targeted, faster update process that does not disturb the stable policy layer above it.
Q24L1 analyst runbook: block IP, reset password, escalate to L2 — which hierarchy level?
✓ Correct Answer: C — A Procedure
A procedure provides specific, step-by-step instructions for performing a defined task or responding to a defined scenario. The runbook tells the analyst exactly what to do, in what order, with no ambiguity — that is the procedural layer. It sits under a standard that defines minimum incident response capabilities, which sits under a policy that requires the organization to be able to detect and respond to incidents. Understanding this hierarchy means understanding that when a runbook does not match the incident in front of you — when the procedure breaks down — the correct response is not to improvise silently but to escalate to a higher tier and flag the gap for the procedure to be updated.
Q25Marketing team storing customer data in personal Dropbox for 6 months — primary risk problem.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Asset outside visibility; cannot be protected or monitored
This is the shadow IT problem, and it directly violates the most fundamental principle of risk management: you cannot protect what you have not inventoried. The data is outside the organization's DLP controls, outside its access management, outside its SIEM coverage, and outside its backup and recovery processes. If Dropbox was breached or the employee's account was compromised, the organization would not know customer data was involved. The data may have already been exposed. Encryption by Dropbox (Option A) does not address the fact that the organization has no visibility, no control, and no audit trail for data it does not know exists in that location.
